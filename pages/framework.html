The Framework

A comprehensive model for preparing educators and students to thrive in an AI rich world.

The Five Core Competencies

These are the essential "Human Operating System" skills that enable individuals to function effectively and responsibly in a world saturated with AI. Click each competency to learn more.

The ability to ask clear, focused, and generative questions to drive learning. In an AI context, this translates to skillful prompting of AI systems, knowing how to phrase questions or tasks to get useful output.

At a deeper level, this is about intellectual curiosity and inquiry based learning. Good questioning is a key component of critical thinking. The ability to formulate questions ties to metacognition, where learners ask themselves what they need to know.

In practice: Students and teachers who master this competency can generate testable questions for investigation, craft effective prompts, and understand how question phrasing affects the answers they receive.

The ability to analyze and evaluate information and outputs from AI with a critical lens. This means detecting errors, biases, misinformation (often called "hallucinations"), and not accepting AI responses as authoritative.

This draws on traditional critical thinking and information literacy. It involves treating AI as a non authoritative source by default, cross checking facts, identifying potential bias or missing perspectives, and recognizing when AI outputs lack logical coherence.

In practice: A critical student will verify an AI generated "fact" against trusted sources. A teacher demonstrating this might say, "ChatGPT's answer sounds confident, but let's inspect its reasoning for flaws."

Understanding and applying principles of ethical behavior when using AI. This includes respecting privacy (not sharing sensitive data with AI tools), awareness of fairness and bias issues, maintaining academic integrity, and acknowledging the broader social impact of AI.

This competency encompasses digital citizenship and moral reasoning. Students must learn to cite AI assistance properly, avoid misrepresenting AI's work as their own, and consider how AI might reinforce stereotypes or exacerbate inequality.

In practice: Students demonstrate academic integrity with AI by including a statement of how they used it. Teachers model ethical use by being transparent about when they use AI in lesson preparation.

Adaptability, continuous learning, and the capacity to manage one's learning in the face of rapid technological change. This aligns with metacognition ("learning how to learn") and lifelong learning skills.

In an AI world, tools and conditions change constantly. Today's ChatGPT might be obsolete next year; a new AI tool might disrupt a profession overnight. Both teachers and students need cognitive flexibility to update their skills and knowledge continuously.

In practice: Students partake in metacognitive activities, keeping learning journals noting when they used AI and how it helped or hindered. Teachers model being learners themselves, openly exploring new tools with their classes.

Valuing and leveraging distinctly human attributes: creativity, curiosity, emotional intelligence, empathy, accountability, and values. This means ensuring humans retain agency and authority over machines.

Students and teachers should maintain a sense of human agency and responsibility over AI outputs and decisions. It's the ability to assert human judgment where AI might offer a shortcut, and to inject human qualities that AI lacks.

In practice: Students iterate on AI generated content and add personal touches or novel ideas. Group projects emphasize collaboration and human creativity. Assessments evaluate the originality of ideas and personal expression, not just final answers.

Teacher Competency Ladder

Teachers develop AI related competencies across three levels. Click each level to explore the capabilities and expectations.

Every teacher should achieve this minimum. The teacher has foundational awareness of AI tools and how they affect learning. They can responsibly use basic AI applications for planning and instruction, and they understand the importance of critical evaluation and ethics.

Capabilities:

  • Understands core AI concepts relevant to education (knows what generative AI is and that it can produce false info)
  • Uses AI for simple tasks: generates quiz questions or lesson outlines, then reviews for accuracy
  • Checks AI outputs carefully before using in class
  • Discusses AI outputs with a skeptical mindset
  • Adheres to ethical guidelines: does not input sensitive student data into AI
  • Recognizes bias in AI outputs and addresses it

Role in Student Learning:

  • Facilitates basic student exposure to AI in a supervised way
  • Ensures student safety and enforces data privacy rules
  • Sets initial norms and communicates class policy on AI use
  • Models a learning spirit and growth mindset about new tools

The teacher integrates AI thoughtfully into pedagogy to enhance learning. They possess solid personal competency in using a range of AI tools and have pedagogical strategies to cultivate students' "Human OS" skills. Most experienced teachers aiming for excellence should reach this level.

Capabilities:

  • Efficiently uses AI to differentiate instruction (generates multiple reading levels, varied problems)
  • Designs assignments requiring students to use AI and apply critical thinking
  • Incorporates AI in formative assessment and uses data to guide teaching
  • Models expert thinking with AI through think alouds and demonstrations
  • Stays up to date on AI developments and updates class practice
  • Can coach colleagues at baseline level

Role in Student Learning:

  • Teaches prompt crafting techniques explicitly
  • Guides student projects with AI, monitoring for actual learning
  • Leads class debates on AI ethics and has students consider biases
  • Evaluates students on their process using AI, not just final answers

The teacher is a leader and innovator in AI integrated education. They deeply understand AI's mechanics and implications and mentor others. Not every teacher needs to reach this level, but having some at this level in each school propels innovation.

Capabilities:

  • Designs original learning activities and curriculum units that leverage AI in transformative ways
  • Possibly creates or customizes AI tools for classroom needs
  • Stays abreast of latest AI research and translates it to practice
  • Mentors peers through workshops, coaching, or as an "AI coordinator"
  • Engages students in metacognitive reflection about AI's effect on their thinking
  • Contributes to policy and advises school or district on AI adoption

Role in Student Learning:

  • Shapes school wide approach to AI literacy across subjects
  • Prepares students to not only use AI but create with it or critique it at societal level
  • Ensures AI use is inclusive and addresses equity concerns
  • Designs new ways to assess Human OS competencies
  • Role models lifelong learning at the highest level
